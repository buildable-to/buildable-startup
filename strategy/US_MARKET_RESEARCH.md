# US Precast Concrete Market Research
### Buildable Internal Document | March 2026

## 1. Market Overview

The US precast concrete market is valued at **$19.77B in 2024** and projected to reach **$32–36B by 2032**, 
growing at a **6.3–6.4% CAGR**. The broader global precast market stands at **$117–150B in 2025**, 
growing toward **$190–260B by 2033**.

Total US construction market: **$2.1 trillion annually** — the largest in the world.

Despite precast being faster, cheaper, and requiring less labor than cast-in-place concrete, 
precast currently represents only **~9% of its potential market share** in overall US construction. 
That gap is the opportunity.

## 3. The Precast Design Bottleneck

### Project Timeline Breakdown (US)

| Phase                                  | Duration         | Notes                                              |
|----------------------------------------|------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| Architectural/Structural Design (A/E)  | 4–6 months       | Before precast manufacturer is involved            |
| Precast Engineering + Shop Drawings    | 2–3 months       | Manufacturer translates design to production dwgs  |
| Shop Drawing Approval Cycles           | 2–6 weeks        | **#1 source of schedule slippage**                 |
| Mold Fabrication                       | ~6 weeks         |                                                    |
| Production/Casting                     | 2–4 weeks/batch  | Elements reach 75% strength within days            |
| Erection                               | Weeks            | ~25,000 sq ft/week for parking garages             |

Precast saves **30%+ of total construction schedule** vs cast-in-place — but only because 
manufacturing and erection are fast. The drawing and approval pipeline has not been optimized.

### Georgia vs US Comparison

| Factor                    | Georgia                          | US                                              |
|---------------------------|----------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Planning % of project     | ~70%                             | ~50–65%                                         |
| Main bottleneck           | Raw drawing creation (manual 2D) | Shop drawing approval cycles + coordination     |
| Manufacturing speed       | Slower (less automation)         | Fast (controlled plants, standard processes)    |
| Erection speed            | Comparable                       | Very fast (~25,000 sq ft/week)                  |
| Revision turnaround       | Days                             | 3+ days per round with outsourced detailers     |

The root cause is the same in both markets: **the paperwork is the constraint, not the building.**

### Engineering Workflow (US Step by Step)

1. **Owner/Architect** — establishes program, aesthetics, layout
2. **Structural Engineer of Record (EOR)** — designs structural system, specifies loads, 
   connection concepts
3. **Precast Manufacturer's Engineering Team** — creates detailed shop drawings 
   (erection plans, piece drawings, embed plans, connection details, rebar schedules)
4. **Approval Cycle** — EOR and architect review shop drawings, issue comments, require 
   revisions. Often **2–4 rounds**. This is where time bleeds.
5. **Production** — Molds, rebar, concrete cast/cured in controlled plant
6. **Logistics** — Sequenced delivery (~60% manufactured before erection begins)
7. **Erection** — Dozens of elements per day

Each precast element = **1–4 drawing sheets**. Large project = **thousands of sheets**.

## 4. Engineering Workforce & Skills Gap

### Documented Shortage of Precast-Trained Engineers

This is not anecdotal. It is confirmed by peer-reviewed research:

- **Auburn University (2024)** — "An Investigation of Critical Barriers Hindering the Use of 
  Precast Concrete Systems in the United States": identifies education deficiency and shortage 
  of specialized designers as **critical barriers** to precast adoption.
  URL: https://cadc.auburn.edu/an-investigation-of-critical-barriers-hindering-the-use-of-precast-concrete-systems-in-the-united-states

- **ASCE Journal of Construction Engineering and Management (peer-reviewed)** — 
  "Factors Affecting the Use of Precast Concrete Systems in the United States":
  *"Education in precast concrete systems in US universities is still deficient. 
  There are shortages of specialized designers."*
  URL: https://ascelibrary.org/doi/10.1061/(ASCE)0733-9364(2008)134:3(169)

- **Idaho State University** — Built an entire precast design studio because 
  *"if asked to design a prestressed girder or a precast pipe, most civil engineers 
  have to learn the design procedures themselves."*

- **PCI Foundation** — Actively funds university fellowships and the Daniel P. Jenny 
  Research Fellowship specifically to address this gap. The industry's own trade organization 
  is funding programs to solve the problem we solve.

### Broader Workforce Data

| Metric                                          | Figure     | Source       |
|-------------------------------------------------|------------|--------------|

## 5. Software Landscape

### How the Market Is Split

**75%+ of mid-tier US precast companies are still primarily working in 2D AutoCAD/DWG.**
BIM and Tekla are concentrated at the top of the market.

#### Tier 1 — Large Fabricators (top ~25%)

| Tool                      | Description                                                                 | Cost                     |
|---------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Tekla Structures**      | Dominant tool. Auto shop drawing generation, IFC/BVBS export, LOD 400+.    | $800–1,600/seat/year     |
| **Autodesk Revit**        | Design-phase coordination. Requires plugins for fabrication detail.         | ~$350/month              |

#### Tier 2 — Mid-Tier & Specialized

| Tool                      | Description                                                                 |
|---------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **AutoCAD**               | Still the primary tool at smaller plants. 2D, manual.                       |
| **ALLPLAN Precast**       | 380+ factories globally (stronger Europe). Auto shop drawing generation.    |
| **StruSoft IMPACT**       | Automated shop drawings with Revit integration.                             |
| **AGACAD Be.Smart**       | Free Revit plugin. LOD 100 → LOD 400 within Revit.                          |

#### Tier 3 — Niche/Legacy

- **BeamWiz / TeeWiz** — Precast beam and double-tee design calculators
- **RebarCAD / CADS RC** — Rebar-specific detailing tools

### BIM Adoption Reality

| Metric                                               | Figure   | Source         |
|------------------------------------------------------|----------|----------------|
| BIM adoption among mid-tier US precast contractors   | **<25%** | BIM Labs       |
| Precast facilities with digital mfg + BIM integrated | **~25%** | BIM Labs       |
| Firms citing interoperability as barrier             | Majority | BIM Labs       |

### AI Tools in Precast Detailing: Zero

- NPCA (Q3 2024): AI in precast is "nascent"
- NPCA Director Hugh Martin: *"AI needs data to begin with — and many precasters don't 
  have tons of data available. The training data is largely nonexistent and disjointed 
  across different systems."*
- Current AI in precast = quality control and concrete mix optimization only
- **Nobody is doing AI for shop drawing generation anywhere in the world**

### File Formats

| Format  | Usage                                                              |
|---------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **DWG** | Primary 2D format. Industry standard shop drawing delivery.        |
| **DXF** | Universal 2D exchange. "Safest bet for editable 2D drawings."      |
| **IFC** | BIM interoperability. IFC4precast exists but not universally used. |
| **PDF** | Common submission format for approval cycles.                      |
| **BVBS**| Bar bending machine data. Embedded in Tekla IFC exports.           |

DWG/DXF are the dominant delivery formats. Most US precast companies are not on 3D BIM.

## 6. The Outsourcing Market — Our Real Competition

US precast companies currently solve the detailing bottleneck by outsourcing to offshore firms. 
This is the incumbent solution we are displacing — not Tekla.

### Offshore Detailing Ecosystem

| Region   | Key Players                                           | Notes                                    |
|----------|-------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| India    | TrueCADD, Advenser, HitechDigital, Silicon Consultant | Dominant. 25+ years serving US clients. |
| Philippines | GPBS (backed by CEG USA)                          | Growing. CEG claims "global leader."     |
| US-based | WBK Engineers, others                                 | Higher cost, faster communication.       |

### Cost Structure

| Item                                   | Cost                          |
|----------------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| US precast drafter hourly rate         | $21–$62/hr (avg **$33/hr**)   |
| Structural shop drawings               | **$80–$200 per sheet**        |
| Outsourced detailing (US-based)        | $40–$80/hr                    |
| Typical 30-hour detailing project      | **$1,200–$2,400**             |
| Indian outsourcing vs US rates         | Est. 40–70% cheaper           |
| Revision turnaround (offshore)         | **3+ days per round**         |

### Why Outsourcing Is Painful (Even If Cheap)

1. **Revision cycles take days** — approval rounds require fast turnaround; offshore doesn't deliver it
2. **No institutional knowledge** — offshore teams learn your templates from scratch each project
3. **Coordination overhead** — timezone, language, file handoff friction
4. **No control** — changes require re-briefing an external team

**Buildable pitch against outsourcing:** Same output. Minutes not days. Full in-house control. 
Instant regeneration on any parameter change.

## 7. Industry Organizations & Market Access

The US precast market is organized through several associations — membership is voluntary, not mandatory.

| Organization | Focus                                          | Scale                        |
|--------------|------------------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| **PCI**      | Structural/prestressed precast (bridges, parking, buildings) | 1,000+ member companies |
| **NPCA**     | Precast products (pipes, manholes, utility)    | 600+ producer members, 420+ certified plants |
| **Regional PCI Chapters** | 14+ regional associations          | State/multi-state level      |

**PCI is our primary channel.** Their members are larger, more sophisticated companies doing 
structural work — our ICP. NPCA skews toward commodity products, less relevant initially.

PCI membership gives access to:
- Annual convention and regional conferences
- eLearning platform and certification programs
- PCI Journal (advertising + research publication)
- Direct member directory and community

**PCI Foundation** (separate nonprofit, ~$1.3–1.9M assets) funds research directly relevant to 
the problems Buildable solves — engineering shortage, education gap, design efficiency. 
Potential partner for credibility and co-publication.

## 9. Market Limitations & Ceilings — Honest Assessment

Precast will not take over all construction. Real structural limits:

| Barrier                          | Detail                                                              |
|----------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Transportation radius**        | ~200–300 miles max before cost advantage disappears. Market is regional, not national. |
| **Design inflexibility**         | Works best for repetitive/standardized designs. Custom architecture = expensive molds. |
| **Seismic zones**                | Lower adoption in California/Pacific NW due to joint concerns.     |
| **High factory capex**           | $15–50M to set up a modern precast plant. Limits supplier density. |
| **Code compliance complexity**   | New ACI/PCI CODE-319 (2025) — US-specific standards, different from SNiP/Eurocode. |
| **Conservative culture**         | "If it ain't broke, don't fix it" — common objection to new tools. |

**Realistic penetration ceiling:** Even in the most precast-mature markets (Nordic countries, Japan), 
precast doesn't exceed 40–45% of total construction. US is at ~9% now. 
The 9% → 30% journey over 10–15 years is our runway. We don't need the ceiling.
